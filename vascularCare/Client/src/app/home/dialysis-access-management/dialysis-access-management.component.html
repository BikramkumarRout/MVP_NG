A Patient Guide to Dialysis
Access Management

Dialysis Relies

on Your Access

For the estimated 468,000 Americans undergoing dialysis, maintaining the vascular access point is critical. Without it, doctors cannot effectively circulate the filtered, dialyzed blood. Mobile Vascular Physicians (MVP) is a concierge vascular and interventional service, staffed by leading doctors. We help you manage your dialysis access point so you continuously and safely undergo dialysis — and avoid hospitalization

Access Types

  • Arteriovenous Graft: A hollow tube is used to connect a vein and artery, usually in your arm or leg.
  • Arteriovenous Fistula: A vascular surgeon artificially connects an artery directly to a vein

Fistulas are longer-lasting and less likely to become infected while grafts are more effective with weak veins and quicker to create. Your MVP physician will advise on the best access type for you

Caring for Your Access

By protecting your access, you can safely continue
dialysis treatments.

  • Regularly wash with soap and water, especially before treatments
  • Avoid infection caused by scratching, coughing, or sneezing on the area
  • Reduce pressure by avoiding tight clothing, sleeping on your access, or carrying heavy objects with that arm
  • Do not use your access arm to check blood pressure, place an IV, or draw blood

Even with careful attention, it’s possible for your access to stop functioning.

Signs and symptoms that your Dialysis Access is not functioning properly

  • Black and blue infiltration
  • Bleeding
  • Decreased trill
  • Difficult or painful cannulation
  • Elevated pressure during dialysis
  • Growing or changing aneurysms
  • Increased pulsatility
  • Poor clearances
  • Redness or swelling
  • Weakness or numbness in access arm

Our number one priority is always for you to return to dialysis as quickly and safely as possible.

If you run into trouble with your access, reach out to your doctor. We are here to help. Your MVP vascular specialist will examine your access and run.

  • Angioplasty: Your doctor inserts a catheter to widen the fistula or graft
  • Thrombectomy: Your doctor removes clots so that your access has good blood flow
  • Stent Placement: Your doctor inserts a permanent tube that holds your fistula or graft open

The average procedure takes 45-60 minutes. We use medications and specialized techniques to ensure it involves the least pain possible and that the procedure will get you back on track with your dialysis — for a longer, healthier, and more comfortable life.
