Hemorrhoidal Disease (HEM)

The tissue and veins around the anal canal are especially sensitive. Too much strain or pressure on the rectal area can irritate the tissue, leading to a hemorrhoid, internal or external bulges that can be itchy and painful.

Hemorrhoids can be found internally (upper anal canal) and externally (under the skin around the anus). The severity of internal hemorrhoids is measured on grades 1-4 based on the level of prolapse (drooping) and whether they can be reduced.

Symptoms

EXTERNAL

External hemorrhoids are usually asymptomatic. However, if the external hemorrhoid clots and becomes thrombosed or if it becomes inflamed, the hemorrhoid can cause painful swelling in the perineal area.

INTERNAL

Depending on the grade and location of internal hemorrhoids, you may experience:

  • Bleeding: Because a hemorrhoid is caused by increased blood supply to the area, bleeding is the most common symptom. Bleeding is even more common once the hemorrhoid has prolapsed through the anal canal.
  • Itching and perineal irritation
  • Fecal soiling: When tissue prolapses, it’s harder to control the anal seal. This can lead to involuntary fecal soiling.

The Superior Rectal Artery
Embolization Procedure

Superior Rectal Artery Embolization is a non-surgical, minimally invasive treatment. The procedure is performed under local anesthesia by an interventional radiologist.

  • Is non-surgical, non-aggressive and non-invasive
  • Requires no general anesthesia
  • Does not create rectal wounds
  • Preserves anal continence
  • Leaves hemorrhoidal tissue intact

Who is Superior Rectal Artery Embolization best for?

It’s the most advantageous treatment for patients with grades 2-3 internal hemorrhoids, for whom medication, non-surgical or surgical treatment, and lifestyle changes were not effective. The team at Mobile Vascular Physicians will perform a CT Angiography to diagnose your hemorrhoid and determine whether you will benefit from this procedure

What is the procedure?

Superior Rectal Artery Embolization is a painless procedure, which takes around 50 minutes from start to finish.

During the procedure, the radiologist places small coils that clot the hemorrhoidal arteries and stop blood flow, simultaneously reducing your symptoms.

  • 1 A thin catheter is inserted to the femoral artery through a tiny 1.5 millimeter opening, which is created in the groin area.
  • 2 The radiologist uses a moving x-ray (fluoroscope) to guide the catheter through the arteries
  • 3 The radiologist places small coils at the branches of the arteries, which seal off the vessels.
  • 4 Depending on the size and location of the hemorrhoid, the radiologist may repeatedly guide the catheter to embolize arteries in more than one area
