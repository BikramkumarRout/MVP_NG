Your guide to treating Lower Urinary Tract
Symptoms from Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia

More than half of all men in their 60s and as many as 90% aged 70-89 years

have some symptoms of benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH, or an enlarged prostate gland.)

The likelihood of developing an enlarged prostate increases with age. As the prostate gets bigger, it may constrict or partly block the urethra, causing Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms (LUTS).

Signs and Symptoms

  • Frequent urination
  • Dribbling at the end of urinating
  • Inability to urinate
  • Incomplete emptying of bladder
  • Incontinence
  • Difficulty starting urination
  • Straining to urinate or weak urine stream

For some patients, these symptoms interfere with sleep, further reducing their quality of life

There is a solution.

Treatment

The interventional radiologist uses X-rays and other advanced imaging to diagnose and treat conditions without surgery.

Because it is targeted treatment, PAE offers many benefits compared to surgical procedures:

  • Shorter hospital stay and recovery time
  • No retrograde ejaculations
  • No erectile dysfunction
  • Low risk of sexual side effects
  • Low risk or urinary incontinence
  • Shorter or no urinary catheterization time
  • Decreased discomfort

What are the risk factors?

Risk factors include:

  • Hematoma at the incision site
  • Blood in the urine
  • Semen, or Stool;
  • Bladder Spasms
  • Infection of the puncture site or prostate

If you would like to treat BPH without surgery, an interventional radiologist will determine if you are a candidate for PAE.

  • The physical exam may include a urine test (urinalysis) and a digital rectal exam.
  • In some cases, a prostate-specific antigen (PSA) test is done to rule out prostate cancer. (Prostate cancer and BPH are not related, but they can cause similar symptoms.)

The PAE Procedure

  • 1 Depending on your radiologist’s procedural protocol, a Foley catheter (thin, hollow tube held in place with a balloon) may be inserted into your urethra to provide a reference point during the procedure. During PAE, you are given local anaesthetic and mild sedation medication, but remain awake
  • 2 PAE is performed through a small incision measuring approximately 3mm in your upper thigh or wrist.
  • 3 The IR uses this incision to insert a catheter into your arteries and guide it near your prostate. Once the catheter is in position, an arteriogram (X-ray with dye) maps the blood vessels feeding the prostate.
  • 4 Next, Embosphere® Microspheres, tiny round particles each about the size of a grain of sand, are injected into the blood vessels, reducing blood supply to your prostate. The radiologist then repositions the catheter and uses the same system to treat the other side of your prostate.
  • 5 Patients may experience “post-PAE syndrome” for days following the procedure, which can include nausea, vomiting, fever, pelvic pain, or painful and frequent urination.
  • 6 In most cases, relief begins to occur within days — as soon as the prostate shrinks, relieving pressure on the urethra and improving symptoms.
